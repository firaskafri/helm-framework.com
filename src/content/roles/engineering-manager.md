## The Shift

What happened to velocity? Agents multiplied individual throughput by three to ten times, and the metric that defined engineering management for a decade turned into noise. Story points lost meaning when an agent could close a backlog's worth of tickets overnight. The classic EM, measured by sprint burndown, standup hygiene, capacity plans, and a steady stream of "unblocked" tickets, was optimizing for a constraint that evaporated.

You're not managing eight engineers anymore. You're managing eight engineers and their agents, each pair producing output that legacy metrics can't interpret. The EM who keeps optimizing for volume is managing the wrong era.

The job moves to what HELM encodes across the organization. **Shift 2 (Leadership)**: stop directing execution, start defining constraints so teams and agents operate inside clear boundaries. **Shift 6 (People systems)**: measure impact, not output volume. **Principle 6 (Team-Wide Adoption Over Individual Mastery)**: the team's effective capability is set by its least-adopted critical-path member, not its star performer. The engineering manager executes those shifts daily: building capability, balancing adoption, and insisting the team measures what actually matters.

## What the Traditional Job Description Looked Like

Every EM posting read the same way. Five-plus years managing engineering teams. Agile and Scrum fluency. Velocity tracking, capacity planning. Strong stakeholder communication. Often a story about scaling headcount from X to Y. Interviews rewarded backlog grooming, sprint planning, and escalation paths. The emphasis was process management: keeping the train on the tracks and the metrics green.
